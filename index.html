About This Site

Welcome! This site is created by students for students at the Budapest University of Technology and Economics (BME), Faculty of Electrical Engineering and Informatics (VIK), Computer Engineering BSc program.

Our goal is to share honest experiences and provide practical advice to help you make the most of your time at BME VIK. Whether you're a prospective student, a freshman, or already deep into your studies, we hope you'll find valuable insights here.

Student Experiences

First Year Journey

Anonymous Student, 2024

The Challenge: The first year was intense. Mathematics, physics, and programming courses all at once can be overwhelming. The transition from high school to university-level thinking was the biggest hurdle.

What Helped: Forming study groups early on made a huge difference. We met regularly to discuss problem sets and prepare for exams together. Don't try to do everything alone!

Key Takeaway: Time management is everything. Start assignments early, attend consultations, and don't be afraid to ask questions during lectures.

Balancing Theory and Practice

Anonymous Student, 2023

The Reality: While the theoretical foundation is strong at BME VIK, practical skills require extra effort outside the curriculum. This is both a challenge and an opportunity.

My Approach: I started working on personal projects and contributing to open-source. This not only improved my coding skills but also helped me understand how theory applies in real-world scenarios.

Laboratory Courses

Anonymous Student, 2023

The Experience: Laboratory courses are where theory meets practice. They can be time-consuming but are incredibly valuable for understanding how things actually work.

Tips: Prepare before each lab session by reviewing the theory and the lab manual. This saves so much time during the actual lab work. Also, make friends with your lab partners - you'll need each other's support.

Advice for Students

üìö Academic Success

  • Attend lectures: Even if you think you can learn from slides alone, being present helps you understand the professor's emphasis and reasoning
  • Use office hours: Professors and TAs are there to help. Don't hesitate to ask for clarification
  • Form study groups: Collaborative learning is more effective and makes studying more enjoyable
  • Start early: Don't wait until the last week before exams to start studying
  • Practice problems: For math and technical courses, solving problems is key to understanding

üíª Technical Skills

  • Learn Git early: Version control is essential. Start using it from day one
  • Practice coding regularly: Consistency beats cramming. Code a little every day
  • Read others' code: Join open-source projects to learn from experienced developers
  • Build projects: Apply what you learn in class to personal projects
  • Learn debugging: Invest time in learning how to use debuggers and logging effectively

  • Take breaks: Your brain needs rest to process information effectively
  • Exercise regularly: Physical activity helps manage stress and improves focus
  • Sleep well: Don't sacrifice sleep for studying. A well-rested mind learns better
  • Join clubs: University isn't just about academics. Make friends and have fun
  • Don't compare: Everyone has their own pace. Focus on your own progress

üéØ Career Preparation

  • Start networking early: Attend industry events, conferences, and meetups
  • Build a portfolio: Create a GitHub profile showcasing your best projects
  • Seek internships: Real-world experience is invaluable and looks great on your CV
  • Learn English: Technical English is essential in the IT industry
  • Stay curious: Technology evolves rapidly. Keep learning new things

üìñ Course-Specific Tips

  • Mathematics: Practice daily, attend problem-solving sessions, and learn to prove theorems step-by-step
  • Programming: Write code yourself. Reading solutions isn't enough - you must practice
  • Electronics: Understand the physics behind components, don't just memorize formulas
  • Digital Systems: Draw diagrams, work through examples manually before using tools
  • Algorithms: Understand the "why" behind each algorithm, not just the implementation

  • Past exams: Practice with previous years' exams to understand the format and difficulty
